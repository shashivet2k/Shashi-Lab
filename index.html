What We Study

Research Areas

Two research pillars — understanding ubiquitination to find and drug critical proteins in disease, and pushing the limits of genome editing precision.

Identifying Druggable Targets

We map E3 ubiquitin ligases and deubiquitinases that control key proteins in cancer and metabolic disease, pinpointing nodes in the ubiquitin network tractable for therapeutic intervention.

Learn more →

Designing Therapeutic Molecules

Using biochemical, proteomic, and cell-based approaches, we develop and validate specific small-molecule inhibitors and peptides to therapeutically target ubiquitin pathway proteins in cancers and metabolic diseases.

Learn more →

Biallelic Genome Editing

We develop improved CRISPR-Cas9 knock-in strategies to efficiently edit both alleles in diploid cells — enabling faithful disease models and exploring therapeutic gene correction.

Learn more →

Ubiquitination in Cancer: Mechanisms and Vulnerabilities

E3 ubiquitin ligases and DUBs are critical regulators of proteins that cancer cells depend on for survival. Current projects investigate USP46 in HPV-driven cervical cancer, CRL4-Cdt2 in breast cancer, and ecDNA amplifications in cervical cancer oncogenesis.

Target Identification and Drug Discovery

Structure-guided drug design and in vitro screening pipelines to develop mechanism-based therapeutics. Current focus: small-molecule inhibitors of USP46 integrating active site structural analysis and compound library screening.

Deubiquitinases in Cardiovascular Physiology

How DUBs regulate the steady-state levels of AT1R and alpha-1D adrenergic receptor, two key determinants of vascular tone and blood pressure — defining ubiquitin-dependent mechanisms in cardiovascular pathophysiology.

CRISPR-Based Genome Editing: From Mechanism to Disease Models

Developing strategies for efficient biallelic knock-in editing in diploid cells to study endogenous proteins, build accurate disease models, and evaluate CRISPR-based correction of pathogenic mutations.
